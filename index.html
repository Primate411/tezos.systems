How Tezos Consensus Works

Tezos uses Tenderbake, a consensus mechanism inspired by classical BFT algorithms. It provides fast finality—once a block is confirmed, it's permanent.

  • Deterministic finality: Transactions final after 2 blocks (~12 seconds)
  • Energy efficient: Proof of Stake, not mining
  • Liquid staking: Delegate without locking tokens
  • Self-amendment: Protocol upgrades via on-chain voting
OpenTezos: Liquid PoS Technical Documentation

Tezos On-Chain Governance

Tezos can upgrade itself without hard forks through on-chain governance. Bakers vote on protocol proposals in a structured 5-period process.

  • Proposal: Bakers submit and upvote proposals
  • Exploration: Top proposal goes to supermajority vote
  • Cooldown: Time for testing and review
  • Promotion: Final confirmation vote
  • Adoption: New protocol activates
OpenTezos: Governance TzKT: Voting History

Tezos Economic Model

Tezos uses Adaptive Issuance—rewards automatically adjust based on staking participation to maintain optimal network security.

  • Dynamic rewards: More staking = lower issuance
  • Target: ~50% staked: System balances security vs liquidity
  • Liquidity Baking: 5 XTZ/min to XTZ/tzBTC DEX pool when active; 0 while the EMA disables the subsidy
  • Burns: Storage fees and failed ops reduce supply
Nomadic Labs: Paris OpenTezos: Economics

Adaptive Issuance

Tezos dynamically adjusts new XTZ issuance based on the staking ratio — the percentage of total supply currently reported as staked by TzKT. The gauge shows the current ratio relative to the 50% target.

  • Below target: Issuance increases to incentivize more staking
  • Above target: Issuance decreases, reducing inflation for everyone
  • At target (~50%): Optimal balance between network security and liquidity
  • Quadratic curve: Adjustments are smooth and gradual — no sudden jumps

This mechanism replaced the old fixed-rate issuance, making Tezos one of the first blockchains with a fully adaptive monetary policy. Bakers and stakers earn higher APY when participation is low, naturally pulling the ratio back toward equilibrium.

Tezos Docs: Adaptive Issuance Nomadic Labs: Paris Announcement

Zero Forks

Tezos has never forked. Unlike most blockchains that split into competing chains when major upgrades happen, Tezos upgrades itself through on-chain governance.

  • Self-amending: Protocol changes are proposed, tested, and voted on-chain by bakers
  • No community splits: Upgrades are consensus-driven — no "Tezos Classic" scenarios
  • 20+ upgrades and counting: From Athens (2019) through Ushuaia (2026), every upgrade adopted seamlessly
  • Why it matters: Forks split communities, liquidity, and developer attention — Tezos avoids all of that
Tezos Docs: Governance Tezos.com: Governance

Tezos Uptime

Tezos mainnet launched on September 17, 2018 and has maintained 100% uptime with zero outages — no downtime, no halts, no emergency restarts.

  • Always online: The chain has never stopped producing blocks since genesis
  • No emergency patches: Unlike some chains that required manual intervention or halts
  • Battle-tested consensus: Tenderbake (now with 6-second blocks) has proven rock-solid
  • Deterministic finality: Blocks are final after 2 confirmations (~12 seconds)
